Biography

Albert Einstein was a German physicist of the 19th and 20th centuries, born on March 14, 1879, and passed away on April 18, 1955. He revolutionized our understanding of the universe with his theory of relativity. Einstein was born in the German city of Ulm, but his family moved to Munich when he was one year old.

At the age of 17, he entered the Federal Polytechnic School of Zurich to study mathematics and physics. After graduation, he obtained Swiss citizenship and began working at the Federal Office of Intellectual Property in Switzerland.

1905 was a pivotal year for Einstein. He published four scientific articles on the photoelectric effect, Brownian motion, the theory of special relativity, and mass-energy equivalence (E = mc²). The first earned him the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1921.

In 1908, he began teaching physics at the University of Bern, later moving to Prague and finally Berlin. The rise of the Nazi regime forced him to leave Germany and move to the United States in 19321. There he taught at the Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton and became an American citizen.

Einstein spent the rest of his life trying to integrate the physical laws of gravity and electromagnetism. He also advocated for pacifist, socialist, and Zionist values. He passed away as a result of an internal hemorrhage on April 18, 1955.

Accomplishments



Albert Einstein made several significant contributions to the world of science. Here are some of his major accomplishments:

1. Empirical Evidence for the Atomic Theory: Einstein provided empirical evidence for the atomic theory through his explanation of Brownian motion.

2. Determination of Avogadro’s Number: He enabled the determination of Avogadro’s number and therefore the size of molecules.

3. Solution to the Photoelectric Effect: Einstein solved the riddle of the photoelectric effect, which couldn’t be explained with Maxwell’s wave theory of light.

4. Special Theory of Relativity: He proposed the special theory of relativity, which brought major changes to mechanics while handling situations close to the speed of light.

These accomplishments laid the foundation for modern physics and changed our understanding of space, time, mass, and energy.


Personal Life



Albert Einstein’s personal life was as complex as his scientific theories. He was married twice, first to Mileva Marić in 1903, with whom he had a daughter and two sons. Their marriage ended in divorce in 1919. In the same year, he married his cousin Elsa Löwenthal, who passed away in 1936.
Einstein was known to have had affairs and received gifts from women, which caused some turmoil in his marriages. Despite his intellectual prowess, Einstein lived much of his life in intellectual solitude. For relaxation, music played a significant role in his life.

Legacy And Impact



Albert Einstein's life and thought left an impact not only on science, but also on philosophy, visual art, and literature. Although he is best remembered for his extraordinary contributions to modern physics, his theory of relativity had perhaps the farthest-reaching implications for thinkers in all fields.
Einstein's work laid the foundation for much of the research into the evolution of the universe as well as modern technology, including lasers and computer chips. His enduring legacy will continue to inspire generations of great thinkers.
